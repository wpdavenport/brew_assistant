# Baltic Porter — Style Doctrine

## Core Philosophy
- Build dense dark-lager richness without stout-like acridity.
- Malt depth should read smooth, layered, and cool-fermented.
- Roast is a framing tool, not the lead note.
- Alcohol can be present, but it must stay integrated and elegant.
- The beer should feel substantial without turning sticky or hot.

## Medal-Oriented Identity
- Deep malt complexity: bread crust, toast, cocoa, dark caramel, restrained dark fruit.
- Lager-clean fermentation with enough softness to keep the beer from tasting hard or thin.
- Roast held below robust porter/stout intensity.
- Smooth bitterness that prevents sweetness from collapsing the finish.
- Long, polished finish that stays drinkable through a judging flight.

## Parameter Doctrine (Competition-Safe Center)
- OG target: 1.072-1.082
- FG target: 1.016-1.022
- ABV target: 7.2-8.6%
- IBU target: 22-32
- SRM target: 24-30
- Apparent attenuation target: 72-80%

These center points keep the beer firmly in 9C while leaving room for richer examples.

## Grain Doctrine
Base:
- 70-90% Munich + Pilsner is a strong traditional-modern hybrid structure.
- Munich should be a real flavor driver, not a token addition.
- Pilsner supports fermentability and keeps the finish from becoming chewy.

Dark malt strategy:
- Use dehusked dark malt for color and soft roast where possible.
- Chocolate malt can support cocoa depth, but keep total roast layered and controlled.
- Special B or a similar dark crystal can add prune/raisin depth in small amounts.

Crystal strategy:
- Keep crystal intentional and restrained.
- One medium crystal plus one dark-fruit crystal is often enough.
- Avoid building sweetness through stacked caramel malts.

Avoid:
- Large roast charges that push into stout.
- Heavy adjunct sweetness.
- Thin base-malt structures that leave the beer tasting like black lager plus roast.

## Hop Doctrine
Bitterness:
- Keep bitterness supportive and smooth.
- Most IBUs should come from the main bittering addition.

Flavor/aroma:
- Late hop character should be low.
- Noble or Polish/Germanic hop families fit well.

Practical template:
- 75-90% of IBUs from 60 min.
- 10-25% from 20-10 min.
- Little to no flameout emphasis.

Avoid:
- Assertive late-hop aroma.
- American citrus/pine expression.
- Harsh bittering that collides with roast.

## Yeast Doctrine
Profile:
- Prefer a clean lager strain with good alcohol handling.
- Fermentation should read smooth and restrained, not fruity-ale-like.

Fermentation control:
- Pitch a large, healthy lager population.
- Start cool and controlled.
- Allow a measured rise for cleanup as attenuation progresses.
- Require a diacetyl check before crash.

Attenuation guardrail:
- A big Baltic Porter cannot finish syrupy.
- If FG is drifting above ~1.022 at normal-strength OG, review mash temp, oxygenation, and yeast health before adding more bittering or roast.

## Water Doctrine
Goal:
- Support dark malt smoothness and mash pH control without turning the beer salty or chalky.

Starting target ranges (RO build-up):
- Ca: 60-100 ppm
- Mg: 5-10 ppm
- Na: 10-50 ppm
- Cl: 70-120 ppm
- SO4: 40-90 ppm
- HCO3: 80-180 ppm

pH:
- Mash pH: 5.35-5.50 at room-temp reading.
- Dark grists often benefit from some alkalinity support, but avoid brute-force baking soda dosing that leaves sodium obvious in the glass.

Avoid:
- Very sulfate-heavy profiles.
- Zero-alkalinity dark-water builds that make roast read sharp.
- Over-sodium water that tastes minerally or saline.

## Mash and Brewhouse Doctrine
Mash:
- Single infusion at 150-153F (65.6-67.2C) is a practical default.
- Lower end for a drier, more elegant finish.
- Upper end only if attenuation is proven and the beer tends to finish too lean.

Boil:
- 60-90 minutes depending on malt bill and Pilsner percentage.
- Strong kettle performance helps dark-malt integration and clarity.

Brewhouse note:
- Big dark lagers expose weak oxygen practice quickly.
- Treat hot-side and cold-side oxygen discipline seriously even before packaging.

## Clarification and Stability Doctrine
- Kettle finings are useful.
- Lager yeast cleanup matters more than aggressive polishing.
- Cold conditioning materially improves integration.
- Oxidation shows up fast as muddy sweetness and stale cocoa/cardboard.

## Packaging and Service Doctrine
Carbonation:
- 2.2-2.5 vols CO2 is a safe competition range.

Service:
- 48-54F (8.9-12.2C) reveals the malt layering better than over-chilled service.

Timeline:
- This style benefits from conditioning.
- Young examples can taste disjointed even when fermentation is technically complete.

## Common Non-Professional Errors
- Treating Baltic Porter like imperial stout with lager yeast.
- Excess roast or ash from dark malt overuse.
- Underpitching or under-oxygenating high-gravity lager fermentation.
- Finishing too sweet and trying to rescue balance with more bittering next time.
- Water that is too soft for mash pH control or too salty from overcorrecting with baking soda.

## Competition Deduction Risks
- Roast drifting toward stout.
- Hot or spirity alcohol.
- Sweet, heavy, under-attenuated finish.
- Thin bitterness support under a large malt bill.
- Lager fermentation faults: sulfur, diacetyl, or incomplete cleanup.
