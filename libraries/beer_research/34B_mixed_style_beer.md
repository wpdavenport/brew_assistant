# Mixed-Style Beer — Style Doctrine

## Core Philosophy
- Mixed-style beer succeeds on integration, not novelty.
- The brewer must know what the base beer is doing and what the twist is adding.
- The best examples still drink like beer, not an ingredients list.
- Judges reward a coherent concept more than raw intensity.

## Medal-Oriented Identity
- Clear declared concept.
- Base style is still recognizable enough to give the beer shape.
- Specialty element is obvious enough to justify the entry.
- Balance and drinkability survive the concept.
- Process execution is clean enough that judges can focus on intent instead of faults.

## Design Doctrine
- Declare the base beer honestly.
- Declare the twist honestly.
- Avoid stacking too many “special” layers unless the beer has already proven it can carry them.
- The further the beer moves from a standard style, the more its internal balance matters.

## Execution Doctrine
- High-gravity mixed-style beers punish weak fermentation.
- Big sugar additions increase variance if process discipline slips.
- Late specialty-hop character should support the concept, not smear it.
- Water should support the beer’s texture and roast/sweetness balance without becoming its own flavor event.

## Common Failure Modes
- Impressive on paper, muddy in the glass.
- Overly sweet finish because the base beer got too big for the yeast plan.
- Harsh roast or bitterness masking the mixed-style concept.
- Entry description failing to tell judges what they are supposed to perceive.
- Inconsistent repeatability because the process is operating with very little margin.

## Competition Deduction Risks
- “Interesting but not harmonious”
- Sweet/hot/alcoholic
- Adjuncts or specialty elements obscuring the base beer
- Concept not matching the declared impression
- Excellent beer entered with weak category framing
