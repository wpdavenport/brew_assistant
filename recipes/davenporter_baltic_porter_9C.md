## CURRENT G40 CALIBRATION NOTE
The original source states `10.0 gal` total water for a `7.0 gal` batch.

- grain bill: 20.63 lb

- pre-boil volume: ~9.26 gal

So the archived `10.0 gal` liquor figure should be treated as original source data, not as a current G40-locked liquor plan.

Most likely explanations:
- the winning batch was brewed on a different system/loss profile
- the batch used a sparge or other top-up not captured in the shorthand recipe
- the original “7 gallon batch” reflects packaged yield rather than strict into-fermenter volume

- Munich-led malt base
- pilsner for fermentability and lift
- restrained but meaningful dark-fruit crystal
- dehusked dark malt for color and smooth roast

- clean lager fermentation

## HISTORICAL MASH AND BOIL
- Mash: 150.0F for 60 minutes
- Boil: 90 minutes
- Mash-out volume recorded: 8.25 gal
- Pre-boil gravity recorded: 1.069

Process interpretation:
- The 150.0F mash is an important balance point here. It gives a beer starting at 1.084 a fair chance to finish rich without becoming sticky.
- The 90 minute boil fits the heavy Munich plus Pilsner structure well and is consistent with building a firm, concentrated dark lager wort.
- The recorded 8.25 gal at 1.069 strongly suggests the original process carried less volume than the current G40 full-volume no-sparge estimate, which helps explain the measured 1.084 OG.

## EXECUTION RISKS
- Underpitching the lager yeast for a dark, moderately strong wort
- Leaving the finish too sweet through weak attenuation
- Overshooting sodium perception if the water profile is reproduced without verifying mash pH and taste outcome
- Letting roast rise above smooth cocoa/bread-crust range
- Relying on this exact fermentation path as if it were clean textbook lager practice; the winning batch appears to have required adaptation midstream

- Roast is present but not dominant.
- The Special B / Cara 60 pairing adds dark-fruit depth without turning the beer into a sweet crystal bomb.
- The hop bill stays out of the way and lets the lager-malt profile carry the beer.
- The recipe is dark and rich enough to feel impressive, but not so aggressive that it stops drinking like Baltic Porter.
- The measured 1.084 to 1.022 performance supports a plush, competition-friendly palate rather than a lean black lager profile.
- The recorded stepwise fermentation schedule shows real process discipline and is a major part of why the beer likely drank cleaner than the raw numbers alone would suggest.
