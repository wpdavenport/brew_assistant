# Davenport ESB - Fuller's Historical Research Notes

Last updated: 2026-03-24

## Purpose
This note tightens the historical and commercial target for Davenport ESB so clone decisions stay anchored to Fuller's rather than to a generic Americanized ESB concept.

## Fuller's As A Brewery
- Beer has been brewed on the Chiswick site for more than 350 years, with Fuller's tracing formal partnership identity to 1845.
- The Griffin name was adopted in 1816 and remains central to the brewery identity.
- Fuller's own history page dates ESB's launch to 1971.
- In 2019, the brewing business and beer brands moved into Asahi UK ownership, while Fuller, Smith & Turner retained the pubs and hospitality business.

## Why Fuller's Matters Historically
- "ESB" was originally a specific Fuller's beer name before American craft brewing broadened it into a generic style term.
- That means a serious Fuller's clone should target the brewery's own balance and house signature, not a generic high-caramel or aggressively hopped "ESB" interpretation.
- Fuller's is one of the breweries that helped define judge expectations for 11C Strong Bitter even though the commercial beer itself predates broad BJCP style codification.

## Fuller's ESB Historical Target
- Fuller's official history gives 1971 as the launch year.
- Secondary brewery-history reporting from Roger Protz and retired Fuller's brewmaster John Keeling indicates ESB began as a stronger winter beer around 1969 and then became a permanent release by 1971.
- The safest interpretation is that the winter-only concept predates the formal full launch.
- Keeling has described ESB as a stronger extension of London Pride.
- Modern official Fuller's bottle notes describe ESB as 5.9% ABV and built from pale, crystal, and chocolate malts with Target, Goldings, Northdown, and Challenger hops.
- Official sensory notes emphasize orange marmalade, soft malt toffee, grassy and peppery hop character, biscuity malt, and a smooth, delicate lingering bitterness.
- Roger Protz reports that ESB was matured over Goldings and then dry-hopped with more Goldings in packaged form; that point is useful as clone direction, but should be treated as high-value secondary reporting rather than current official process disclosure.

## Fuller's House Signature
- Fuller's states that its house yeast contributes orange-citrus and toffee flavors, with marmalade becoming more discernible at higher ABV.
- This reinforces that the orange-marmalade signature is not just a hop effect; it is also a fermentation effect.
- A clone that gets the hops roughly right but ferments too clean will miss the target.

## Clone-Relevant Process Implications
- Keep the beer clearly English and cask-ale-derived in balance: rich but still drinkable, rounded but not sweet, expressive but not fruity in a generic way.
- Hop selection should stay inside the known Fuller's family: Target, Northdown, Goldings, and Challenger are historically and commercially appropriate.
- Late-hop character should support marmalade, pepper, grass, and floral English expression rather than modern bright citrus.
- Malt should not be built like an American ESB with heavy crystal stacking. Fuller's official cue is simpler: pale + crystal + chocolate, with biscuit character emerging from the total system rather than from a novelty grist.
- The house yeast character is crucial. 1968/WLP002-class fermentation remains the right clone direction despite its operational difficulty.

  - CB 34.5
- pale malt from Simpsons in both tuns
- crystal malt totaling roughly 1,150 kg across both tuns

- gypsum added in the mash/tuns

Clone implications:
- The hop family used in Davenport is confirmed as directionally correct.
- The brewery grist is simpler than many homebrew Fuller's clones suggest.
- Chocolate malt is present, but at trace level only; it functions as a color/edge tool, not a chocolate flavor signature.
- The strongest authenticity move is therefore simplification, not extra specialty-malt layering.

## Davenport Implications
- Current Davenport hop selection is historically defensible because it uses three of the four officially listed Fuller's ESB hop varieties: Target, Northdown, and Goldings.
- The updated Davenport hop bill should include all four visible brewery hops for maximum fidelity.
- The one ingredient family that deserves the closest scrutiny is the malt bill. The direct brew-sheet evidence strongly favors pale + crystal + trace chocolate over amber-led homebrew interpretation.
- For a "closest possible" homebrew clone, simplifying the grist is more justified than adding more nuance.

## Practical Historical Conclusion
- Davenport is already pointed in the correct direction because it respects Fuller's yeast character, English hops, moderate bitterness, and soft-firm finish.
- The biggest remaining clone question is not "are the hops English enough?" but rather "is the malt architecture reproducing Fuller's mahogany/toffee/biscuit signature as faithfully as possible?"
